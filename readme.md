## Activity 2: Team Contract (35 min)

A team contract is not busywork. It is insurance. When conflicts arise in Week 7 or Week 12, you will reference this document. Every high-performing team in last semester's cohort had a strong contract. Every team that imploded did not.

### Instructions:
1. Copy `templates/team-contract.md` into your repo at `team/team-contract.md`
2. Fill in every section together. Do not split it up. Every team member must be present for every decision.
3. Pay special attention to:
   - **Roles:** Who is Program Lead? Discovery Lead? Tech Lead? (See role descriptions in the template)
   - **Decision rules:** How do you handle disagreements? Majority vote? Lead decides? Consensus required?
   - **Communication norms:** What tool? How fast do you respond? What counts as "blocking"?
   - **Contribution tracking:** How will you log who did what? (This directly affects your peer assessment scores)
4. Every team member signs the contract (add your name, GitHub username, and date at the bottom)
5. Commit and push the signed contract before the end of this activity

### Why this matters:
The syllabus states that peer assessments happen at midterm (Week 9) and final (Week 15). Those assessments can adjust your individual grade by up to 10% of the team score. Your contract is the reference document for those assessments. Write it like it matters, because it does.

### By the end of this activity, you must have:

## Activity 3: Individual Problem Brainstorm - The 3H Method (35 min)

Before your team decides what to build, each member generates ideas independently. This prevents groupthink and ensures the team has a rich pool of ideas to select from next week.

### The 3H Method

You will generate exactly **3 problem statements**, one from each category:

| Category | Prompt | Think About |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| **H1: Hassle** | What process in your daily life is tedious, error-prone, or needlessly manual? | Scanning receipts, reading PDFs, organizing photos, tracking expenses, translating menus, reading handwritten notes |
| **H2: Hardship** | What problem do you see in your community or country that affects real people? | Healthcare access, education gaps, language barriers, document processing for government services, accessibility, local business struggles |
| **H3: Horizon** | What new capability does multimodal AI unlock that was impossible 2 years ago? | Apps that see and understand images, read any document, process video, analyze handwriting, understand charts, translate signs in real time |

### Rules for this activity:
- Work **alone and in silence** for the first 20 minutes
- Write on paper first, not on a screen
- Each problem statement must follow this format:

```
WHO: [specific person or group - not "people" or "users"]
PROBLEM: [what they struggle with, in one sentence]
CURRENT SOLUTION: [how they handle it today, even if badly]
AI ANGLE: [which AI capability from this week's lecture could help]
WHY IT MATTERS: [what changes if this problem gets solved]
```

- Do NOT think about solutions yet. Do NOT think about feasibility yet. Just identify real problems with real people.
- Use the capabilities list from Thursday's lecture (slide 6) as a reference for the AI ANGLE field

### After 20 minutes of silent writing:
- Spend the remaining 15 minutes refining your problem statements
- Add detail to the WHO field (make it more specific)
- Add detail to the AI ANGLE field (name the exact capability, not just "AI")
- You may look at your lecture notes and the `resources/ai-capabilities-reference.md` file
- You may quietly ask a neighbor a clarifying question, but do NOT share your ideas yet

### By the end of this activity, you must have:
- [ ] 3 problem statements written on paper (H1, H2, H3)
- [ ] Each statement has all 5 fields filled in (WHO, PROBLEM, CURRENT SOLUTION, AI ANGLE, WHY IT MATTERS)

You will NOT share these with your team today. That happens in Lab 2 next week. This is deliberate: giving your ideas a week to breathe leads to better refinement.
